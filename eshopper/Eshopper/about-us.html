About Us

A team of psychologists and professionals conducted a survey understanding how people reflect internal feeling affectively in different circumstances. They collected information of affective reactions from 5,000 people of 32 countries.

The findings showed that the most expressive people are Africans (0.523). Americans were positioned the second (0.519), Australians the third (0.510). All these people have got marks higher than 0.5. Indian, Chinese, Japanese, South Korean, German and Indonesian got marks lower than 0.5, and Hong Kong people got the lowest mark 0.399, positioned 32, the last in the survey, ie. According to this survey, HK people do not show explicitly of what they feel inside.

Researchers’ analysis indicated that areas with more immigrants, with diversified culture and languages, require people to have more facial expressions to show their emotions, while in areas with more uniform culture and few immigrants, people need not be so expressive in communicating with others.

Quite a number of literature has documented that showing or communicating our internal emotion in proper ways is healthy behaviour, quality of emotional intelligence.

As what I shared in previous assemblies, professionals believe that IQ cannot predict accurately who will be successful in life. The key characteristic of successful people is emotional intelligence, EQ.

Two of the major domains for emotional intelligences are:

  1. Knowing one’s emotions, recognizing your feelings such as happiness, pride, worry, anger etc.
  2. Managing emotions, ability to express happiness and love; ability to soothe worry or control anger and to shake off anxiety, face sadness in life.

We have to decently show and share our positive emotions such as happiness, love, pride, gratitude, appreciation; we also need to manage our negative emotions such as anger, frustration, sadness and anxiety.

When we are happy we have to smile and share; when we feel thankful and grateful to somebody, we have to show our appreciation by words and by actions like what teachers have shared on filial piety. On the other hand, when we are angry or unhappy, we have to share and vocalize our feeling to communicate with others and to listen to people with different opinions.

Despite that, all negative feelings have to be expressed in civilized and healthy ways. In a recent incident, some people rushed into a venue of debate competition prize giving ceremony, shouting hysterically, interrupting speakers and hindering the progress of the function. This is uncivilized act and against the generally-accepted core value of respect and courtesy. In the same vein, in a recent school fund raising activity for the Nepal Earthquake victims, when we donated money, we should not throw notes and coins from the upper storeys onto the ground or into the donation box. It was disrespectful, uncivilized and against common social etiquette.

Emotional competencies are not inborn, but can be learned, improved and practiced, especially in childhood. And I believe young people like you can also practice emotional intelligence more in our everyday life.

If you want to be well accepted, socially comfortable when getting along with others, practice outgoing character, cheerful and courteously carry yourself. I believe we, human beings, a kind of social animal, would feel happy and jolly whenever we are and wherever we go.
